Buying Guide: What to Look for in an Automatic Cat Feeder

1. Portion Control Accuracy

Not all feeders dispense the exact amount every time. Look for feeders with adjustable portion increments (1/8 cup or smaller) and a reputation for consistency. We found that gravity-fed designs (like the WOPET) tend to be less precise than auger-based systems (like PetSafe).

2. Power Source & Backup

Most feeders run on AC power with battery backup. We recommend a feeder that uses at least 4 D batteries for backup (longer life) rather than 3 AA (shorter life). If you travel a lot, choose a model with a battery backup that lasts weeks.

3. Connectivity & Features

Wi-Fi feeders let you schedule and monitor from anywhere. Consider if you need voice recording, camera (rare), or slow feed. If you only want simple scheduling, a non-Wi-Fi feeder like the WOPET is fine.

4. Food Type Compatibility

Most automatic feeders work only with dry kibble. For wet food, look for a compartment-style feeder (Cat Mate) or a refrigerated unit (expensive). Avoid putting wet food in a gravity feeder—it can clog and spoil.

5. Ease of Cleaning

Removable bowl and hopper are essential. Stainless steel bowls are better than plastic for hygiene. Some feeders (like SUREFEED) have fixed bowls that are harder to clean.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Can I use an automatic cat feeder for wet food?
Most automatic feeders are designed for dry kibble only. Wet food can spoil and clog the mechanism. If you need to feed wet food, look for compartment-style feeders with built-in ice packs (like the Cat Mate C5000) or refrigerated units. Always test wet food storage time before relying on it for a weekend trip.
Q: How long do batteries last in automatic cat feeders?
Battery life varies by model. Wi-Fi feeders drain batteries faster—expect 2–4 weeks with 4 D batteries in backup mode. Non-Wi-Fi feeders like the WOPET can last 2–3 months on 3 AA batteries. Always use high-quality alkaline or lithium batteries for backup.
Q: Will my cat be able to break into the feeder?
Some clever cats learn to paw the lid or push the feeder over. Look for models with locking lids, heavy bases, or anti-jam mechanisms. We found the WOPET’s lid is easy to pop open, but the PetSafe and SUREFEED have secure closures. If your cat is a determined escape artist, consider a feeder with a microchip lock.
Q: Do I need a Wi-Fi enabled feeder if I’m just going to work?
Not necessarily. If you only need to set a few meals on a timer, a basic programmable feeder without Wi-Fi works fine. Wi-Fi is useful if you want to adjust feeding times remotely, monitor portion history, or get notifications if the feeder jams. It also helps if your schedule changes frequently.
Q: How many times a day should I feed my cat with an automatic feeder?
Veterinarians recommend at least 2–3 meals per day for adult cats, but kittens and diabetic cats may need more small meals. Most automatic feeders allow 4–6 scheduled meals, which is ideal. Avoid feeding less than two times a day, as cats can regurgitate if they eat too fast after long gaps.
