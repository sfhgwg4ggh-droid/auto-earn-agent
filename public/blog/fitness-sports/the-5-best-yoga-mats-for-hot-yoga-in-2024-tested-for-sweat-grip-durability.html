Buying Guide: What to Look for in a Hot Yoga Mat

Before we dive into the reviews, here are the key factors we evaluated:

  • Grip (Wet & Dry): The mat should feel stickier when wet, not slippery. Look for materials like natural rubber, polyurethane (PU) top layer, or microfiber suede.
  • Thickness: Hot yoga often involves kneeling and balancing. We recommend 4–6mm for portability and cushioning. Thicker mats (6mm+) can be too spongy for balance poses.
  • Material: Eco-friendly options (natural tree rubber, jute, cork) offer great grip but require care. PVC mats are durable but less breathable. We prefer open-cell tops that absorb sweat for traction.
  • Size: Standard 68"x24" works for most, but tall practitioners may need 72" or 74" lengths. Extra width (26") helps with wide-legged poses.
  • Maintenance: Hot yoga mats need frequent cleaning. Look for antimicrobial properties or machine-washable covers.

FAQ

How do I clean my hot yoga mat?

Most mats can be wiped down with a gentle spray (water + vinegar or a tea tree oil solution) after each use. For open-cell mats (Jade, B Mat), avoid soaking — just spray and wipe. For PVC mats (Manduka, Gaiam), you can use a mild soap solution. Never machine wash unless the manufacturer says it’s okay.

Should I use a towel on my mat for hot yoga?

It depends on your mat. If you have a closed-cell mat like Manduka Pro, a towel can help absorb sweat and prevent slipping. If you have an open-cell or PU-top mat (Liforme, Jade), the towel may actually reduce grip — the mat is designed to get stickier when wet.

What’s the best thickness for hot yoga?

We recommend 4–5mm. Thinner mats (1–3mm) offer better feel for balance but less cushioning for knees. Thicker mats (6mm+) can feel unstable in standing poses. 5mm is the sweet spot for hot yoga.

Are cork yoga mats good for hot yoga?

Cork mats are naturally antimicrobial and non-slip when dry, but they can become slippery when wet unless treated. We didn’t include one in our top picks because most cork mats require a towel or spray to maintain grip. They are eco-friendly, though, and can work if you don’t sweat excessively.
