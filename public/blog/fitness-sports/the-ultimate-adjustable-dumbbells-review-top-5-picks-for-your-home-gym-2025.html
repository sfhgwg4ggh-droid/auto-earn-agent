Why Choose Adjustable Dumbbells?

Adjustable dumbbells are a game-changer for home gyms. They combine the versatility of a full weight set into a compact footprint. You can go from a light warm-up to a heavy squat or bench press in seconds. Plus, they're safer than dropping iron plates and much easier to store. We've seen them become the backbone of thousands of home setups, and for good reason.

In-Depth Reviews of the Top Adjustable Dumbbells

1. Bowflex SelectTech 552 – The All-Rounder

We put the Bowflex SelectTech 552 through its paces for two weeks. The dial mechanism is smooth and intuitive—just turn the dial to your desired weight and lift. It covers 5 to 52.5 pounds per dumbbell in 5-pound increments. You can also buy an add-on kit to get 2.5-pound increments, which we recommend for progressive overload. The build quality is solid, but the plastic handle grips feel a bit thin. At full weight, the dumbbell is quite long (over 15 inches), which can make certain exercises awkward. Still, it's one of the most popular models for a reason: it's reliable, widely available, and holds its value. Check price on Amazon.

2. PowerBlock Elite EXP 50 – The Space Saver

PowerBlock dumbbells look like a Transformer, but they work incredibly well. The Elite EXP 50 ranges from 5 to 50 pounds in 2.5-pound increments (up to 25 lbs) and 5-pound increments beyond that. The adjustment is quick: you slide a pin into the desired weight plate. The dumbbells are very compact—they don't get longer as you add weight—so they feel much like traditional dumbbells. We particularly like the safety design: the plates are fully enclosed, so you won't drop them. The downside? The grip is rectangular rather than round, which takes getting used to. Also, the max weight of 50 lbs may be limiting for advanced lifters. Check price on Amazon.

3. NordicTrack SpeedWeight iSelect – The Tech-First Option

The NordicTrack SpeedWeight iSelect uses a motorized dial that changes weight with a push of a button. It looks sleek and modern, and we loved the fast adjustment. The set comes with a sturdy stand, and the digital display shows the weight and tracks reps. However, we found the unit bulky and heavy—the base weighs over 60 pounds. The weight range is 10 to 50 pounds in 5-pound increments, but the lack of lighter weights (e.g., 5 lbs) is a bummer for beginners. There have been some reports of electronic failures after a few months. We'd recommend this only if you want that flashy tech experience and don't mind a higher price tag. See at Walmart.

4. Core Fitness Adjustable Dumbbell – The Budget Pick

If you're on a tight budget, the Core Fitness set offers decent quality for under $300. It adjusts from 5 to 34 pounds per dumbbell in 5-pound steps. The mechanism is similar to the Bowflex—a dial selector—though it's not quite as smooth. The dumbbells are compact and easy to handle. However, the plastic tray that holds the weights feels cheap, and the max weight is low for anyone past the beginner stage. We'd recommend this for light home workouts, seniors, or beginners. Check price on Amazon.

5. Snode AD80 – The Heavy Lifter's Dream

Finally, the Snode AD80 is a beast. Each dumbbell goes from 11 to 88 pounds in 5.5-pound increments. The build is all-metal, and the adjustment mechanism uses a rotating handle—similar to the Bowflex but much heavier-duty. We tested this with bench presses and rows, and it felt rock-solid. The smaller increments are great for progressive overload. The catch? It's expensive (around $500–$650) and very large at full weight. If you're an intermediate to advanced lifter who needs heavy weights and fine gradations, this is our top recommendation. Check price on Amazon.

Buying Guide: How to Choose the Best Adjustable Dumbbells

Consider the Weight Range

Think about your current strength and goals. Beginners might be fine with 5–50 lbs per dumbbell, while intermediate lifters will want at least 70–80 lbs. Advanced users should look at models like Snode AD80 or PowerBlock EXP Pro.

Check the Increments

Smaller increments (2.5–5 lbs) allow for more gradual progress. Some models offer optional add-on kits to get smaller steps. Don't skip this—it's crucial for muscle growth.

Evaluate Build Quality

Plastic handles or trays can feel cheap and may not last. Look for metal components, especially in the weight selector mechanism. The best adjustable dumbbells are built like tanks.

Think About Space and Storage

Most adjustable dumbbells come with a tray or stand. Measure your floor space. Some models (like the PowerBlock) are very compact, while others (like Bowflex or Snode) get long when loaded.

Set a Budget

Good adjustable dumbbells range from $250 to over $600. Don't cheap out too much—cheap mechanisms can break. The sweet spot is $350–$500 for a durable set that will last years.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are adjustable dumbbells worth the money?

Absolutely. They replace an entire set of dumbbells and save space. For most home gyms, a good pair pays for itself within a year compared to buying multiple fixed-weight pairs.

Can you drop adjustable dumbbells?

Not really. Most models (especially Bowflex and Core) are not designed for dropping. PowerBlock and Snode are a bit sturdier, but dropping any adjustable dumbbell can break the mechanism or damage the floor. Use them on a rubber mat.

How long do adjustable dumbbells last?

With proper care, 5–10 years is common. Higher-end models with metal parts last longer. Budget models with plastic components may wear out faster. Always store them in the tray when not in use.

What is the best adjustable dumbbell brand?

Bowflex and PowerBlock are the most trusted. Bowflex is great for all-round use, while PowerBlock excels in compactness and safety. For heavy lifters, Snode is excellent.

Do adjustable dumbbells feel like regular dumbbells?

They feel close, but not identical. Bowflex and Core Fitness have a more natural shape. PowerBlock's rectangular grip feels different. We recommend trying them in a store if possible, or ordering from a place with a good return policy.
